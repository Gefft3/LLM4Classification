                                    <p><span style="font-weight: 400;">While Wisconsin is in the midst of another surge in COVID-19 cases more than four out of five people are not up to date in getting the most recently available vaccine to prevent serious illness.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Recommendations to mask when in larger indoor crowds are returning. On Wednesday, the Wisconsin Council of Churches told its 2,000 member congregations in an email message that the council “strongly encourages congregations to return to using masks in worship and other gatherings.” The council is an ecumenical organization representing 21 Christian faith traditions and about 1 million church members.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Public health officials don’t track the total number of COVID-19 cases any more, primarily because many of the tests that confirm an infection are done at home. Nevertheless, “our surveillance systems are all indicating that we’re at high activity for COVID-19,” said Tom Haupt, respiratory disease epidemiologist for the Wisconsin Department of Health Services (DHS).</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Measures of the surge include increased hospitalizations and an increased number of cases that have come to the attention of public health officials.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">As of Jan. 6, about 530 people</span><a href="https://www.dhs.wisconsin.gov/covid-19/hosp-data.htm" target="_blank"> <span style="font-weight: 400;">were hospitalized</span></a><span style="font-weight: 400;"> in Wisconsin with COVID-19, DHS reported, and about 13% of those patients were in intensive care. Hospitalizations have increased in four out of the state’s seven health care regions.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Of the laboratory test results for COVID-19 that have been reported to DHS, about 13% have been positive, Haupt told the Wisconsin Examiner Thursday. Tests for influenza and respiratory syncytial virus (RSV) have also come back positive about 13% of the time, he said.</span></p>

<div class="single-blog-content entry wpex-mt-20 wpex-mb-40 wpex-clr" itemprop="text"><p>Late last week, <a href="https://www.reuters.com/business/healthcare-pharmaceuticals/bird-flu-infects-commercial-us-poultry-flock-first-time-since-april-2023-10-06/">Reuters writer Tom Polansek</a> reported that, “<strong>The U.S. has detected its first case of avian flu on a commercial poultry farm since April</strong>, in a flock of 47,300 turkeys in Jerauld County, South Dakota, the U.S. Department of Agriculture (USDA) said.</p>
<p class="text__text__1FZLe text__dark-grey__3Ml43 text__regular__2N1Xr text__small__1kGq2 body__full_width__ekUdw body__small_body__2vQyf article-body__paragraph__2-BtD" data-testid="paragraph-1">“Infected flocks are <strong>culled</strong> to prevent the spread of the virus, potentially tightening the poultry meat and egg <strong>supply</strong> if more cases occur.</p>
<p class="text__text__1FZLe text__dark-grey__3Ml43 text__regular__2N1Xr text__small__1kGq2 body__full_width__ekUdw body__small_body__2vQyf article-body__paragraph__2-BtD" data-testid="paragraph-2">“Since 2022, <strong>58.8 million</strong> U.S. chickens, turkeys and other birds have been<strong> wiped out by the disease</strong>, officially known as highly pathogenic avian influenza (HPAI), according to the USDA. The <strong>losses drove prices </strong>for turkey meat and eggs to <strong>record highs last year</strong>, raising costs for inflation-hit consumers.”</p>
<p data-testid="paragraph-2">The Reuters article noted that, “Cal-Maine Foods, the <strong>biggest</strong> U.S. egg producer, said this week that <strong>the average price of conventional eggs dropped 48% from last year to $1.24 per dozen in a quarter that ended on Sept. 2</strong>. The company’s total net sales <strong>fell 30%</strong> to $459.3 million in the quarter.”</p>
<p data-testid="paragraph-2">Polansek added that, “Prior to this week’s outbreak, U.S. infections <strong>were limited</strong> to live bird markets and ‘non-poultry’ birds since April, USDA records show. The last commercial farms infected in April raised turkeys in South Dakota and North Dakota, records show.”</p>
<p data-testid="paragraph-2"><a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2023/10/05/business/france-duck-vaccination.html?searchResultPosition=4">Emma Bubola</a> reported in Friday’s New York Times that, “In recent years the disease, known as Highly Pathogenic Avian Influenza, or HPAI, has spread through poultry farms <a class="css-yywogo" title="" href="https://www.nytimes.com/2023/08/30/science/birds-flu-antarctica.html">across the globe</a>, causing the deaths of <a class="css-yywogo" title="" href="https://www.aphis.usda.gov/aphis/ourfocus/animalhealth/animal-disease-information/avian/avian-influenza/hpai-2022/2022-hpai-commercial-backyard-flocks" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">nearly 60 million farmed birds</a> in the United States alone.”</p>
<p data-testid="paragraph-2">Meanwhile, <a href="https://www.wsj.com/livecoverage/stock-market-today-dow-jones-10-04-2023/card/the-egg-price-narrative-gets-scrambled-LDwUxxw8dncmTBC8kJzf?mod=Searchresults_pos10&amp;page=1">Wall Street Journal writer Aaron Back</a> reported last week that, “<strong>Cal-Maine reported [earlier this month] that egg production has recovered from the avian flu outbreak of 2022</strong>, which <a href="https://www.wsj.com/articles/worst-avian-flu-in-u-s-history-is-hitting-poultry-wild-birds-even-bears-11674366891">caused the deaths</a> of nearly <strong>58 million</strong> poultry in 47 states. <strong>Egg prices have collapsed as a result</strong>. Cal-Maine said its average selling price for eggs fell to $<strong>1.59</strong> a dozen in its fiscal first quarter, compared to <strong>$2.28</strong> a year earlier.</p>
<div class="e141zjhk1 css-xm7a9b-ParagraphContainer">
<p class="e141zjhk0 css-fzd6vc-FormattedText">“<strong>The company’s revenue in the quarter fell 30% from a year earlier</strong>. At the same time, though, labor and other costs have remained elevated, including expenses incurred to guard against future avian flu outbreaks. As a result, it posted an <strong>operating loss of $6.76 million</strong>, compared with an <strong>operating profit of $163.9 million a year earlier</strong>.</p>

This would make the 2023 total 7.885 billion dozen table eggs, down 48 million from last month’s projection. <strong>This is still an increase of 1.3 percent from 2022</strong>.”</p>
<p>Elsewhere, <a href="https://www.wsj.com/articles/corn-futures-drop-as-harvest-outlook-outweighs-geopolitics-daily-grain-highlights-90ce8026?mod=Searchresults_pos1&amp;page=1">Dow Jones writer Kirk Maltais</a> reported yesterday that, “<a href="https://www.usda.gov/oce/commodity/wasde">Thursday’s WASDE report</a> is <strong>expected to show the USDA revising its production outlooks to the downside, albeit slightly</strong>.</p>
<p>“U.S. corn production is <strong>expected to drop 34 million bushels to 15.1 billion bushels</strong>, while soybean production is <strong>expected to decline 14 million bushels to 4.13 billion bushels</strong>, according to analysts surveyed by The Wall Street Journal.</p>
<p>“Analysts expect the report to <strong>point toward a solid finish</strong> for U.S. crops.”</p>

<p>“Commodity brokerage StoneX, however, raised its estimate of U.S. 2023 corn production to <strong>15.282</strong> billion bushels from 15.102 billion and lifted its forecast for U.S. soybean production to <strong>4.175</strong> billion bushels from 4.144 billion.</p>
<p>“<strong>CBOT corn</strong> is up around 4% from a 33-month low of $4.67-3/4 last month and <strong>soybeans</strong> are near their lowest since December 2021.”</p>

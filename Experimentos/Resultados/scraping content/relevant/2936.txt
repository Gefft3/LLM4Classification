<h2>Background</h2>

<p>Of the four genera present in the virus family Arenaviridae, <em>Mammarenavirus</em> is the only genus that infects mammals. The genus is divided into the New World arenaviruses and the choriomeningitisLassa virus serocomplex, distributed worldwide. Some New World arenaviruses, such as the Junin, Chapare, Guanarito, Machupo, and Sabia viruses, cause hemorrhagic fever in humans.</p>

<p>While the pathogenic arenaviruses are generally transmitted to humans through infected rodents, <em>Tacaribe mammarenavirus,</em> found in bats and not considered infectious to humans, is anecdotally known to cause influenza-like symptoms. Although bats are known to host zoonotic viruses, there is limited epidemiological evidence on the association between bats and arenaviruses, consisting mostly of a single isolated virus and experimental infections of bats.</p>

<p>However, since Tacaribe virus belongs to the same clade as the hemorrhagic fever-causing viruses that also exhibit immune evasion and cellular tropism, the possibility of severe infections from Tacaribe virus cannot be ruled out.</p>

<h2>About the study</h2>

<p>In the present study, the researchers collected bat samples from southeastern Brazil and analyzed spleen, lung, intestine, kidneys, and liver tissues from the samples. They collected a total of 1,047 bats from 32 species and analyzed 3670 tissue samples. To detect arenavirus RNA, they performed reverse transcription polymerase chain reaction (RT-PCR) with primers modified to amplify partial sequences of the New World arenaviruses RNA-dependent RNA polymerase gene.</p>

<p>Furthermore, they used Illumina-based deep sequencing to obtain complete coding sequences of arenaviruses from two of the collected bat species. The sequences obtained from the RT-PCR and the complete coding sequences were used for phylogenetic analyses and to determine the homogenous sequence distances.</p>

<h2>Results</h2>

<p>The results reported arenavirus RNA from 1.62% (17 out of 1047) of the sampled bats belonging to three species  one <em>Artibeus planirostris</em>, four <em>Artibeus lituratus,</em> and 12 <em>Carollia perspicillata</em>. The viruses from the two <em>Artibeus</em> species were identified as <em>Tacaribe mammarenavirus</em>, while the virus detected in the 12 <em>Carollia perspicillata</em> samples was identified as a novel species, which the authors called <em>Tiet mammarenavirus</em>.</p>

<p>All the samples were collected from forested and urban areas within 60 km of each other, indicating a possible viral reservoir in bats in the region. Similar viral concentrations were found in tissues from the spleen and lungs, suggesting a systemic infection. The highest arenavirus concentration was found in an intestine tissue sample, which suggests enteric <a href="/health/What-is-Viral-Shedding.aspx" class="linked-term">viral shedding</a>.</p><div id="mobile-middle-mrec-promo-spot" class="mobile-promo-wrap mobile-promo-mrec">

<p>The phylogenetic reconstruction based on the partial RNA-dependent RNA polymerase gene sequences retrieved two New World arenaviruses clades in Brazil. The arenaviruses from the two <em>Artibeus </em>species formed one clade, while the sequences from the <em>Carollia perspicillata </em>samples formed another clade. The complete coding sequences revealed identical genome organization as other mammarenaviruses and formed a monophyletic group with Tacaribe virus that was well-supported.</p>

<p>However, the phylogenetic analysis using the complete small segment genes showed a sister relationship between the Ocozocoautla de Espinosa virus and the clade with the Brazillian sequences and Tacaribe virus sequences.</p>

<p>The Ocozocoautla de Espinosa virus is thought to have caused an outbreak of hemorrhagic fever in Mexico. Furthermore, the recombination analyses and the sequence distances did not indicate any recombination events.</p>

<p>The taxonomic assessment of the new virus species from the <em>Carollia perspicillata</em> samples was based on pairwise sequence comparisons with Tacaribe virus that indicated a nucleotide sequence identity of less than 80% and a nucleocapsid protein amino acid identity of 88.6% to 90% and the presence of a distinct host. The species was named after a river located near the sampling site.</p>

<h2>Conclusions</h2>

<p>To summarize, the study investigated bat species collected from southwestern Brazil for the presence of arenavirus RNA using sequence amplification methods. The results reported arenavirus RNA presence from three species of bats comprising 1.62% of the total sample size.</p>

<p>The phylogenetic analyses revealed that the arenaviruses detected from the bat samples are closely related to the Tacaribe virus, a New World arenavirus found in bats. One of the detected arenaviruses is a novel species and was given the name <em>Tiet mammarenavirus.</em></p>

<p>While New World arenaviruses are generally found in rodents, the results from this study indicate that bats could be arenavirus reservoirs and need to be explored further.</p><div id="mobile-bottom-promo-spot" class="mobile-promo-wrap mobile-promo-leaderboard-320">
